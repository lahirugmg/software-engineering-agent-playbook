# Skill: innovation-discovery

**Type:** atomic

## Purpose

Explore unspoken user needs, unserved market opportunities, and problems worth solving before a ticket or requirement exists. Surfaces what to build next, before the question “what should we build?” has been formally asked.

## When to Invoke

- A product area feels stagnant and the team needs to identify the next meaningful thing to build.
- Users are churning or not adopting a feature and the reason is unknown.
- A strategic bet is being considered and its underlying assumptions need to be tested.
- Innovation time is available and there is no obvious next thing on the backlog.

## Workflow

1. **Define the discovery question.** Innovation discovery is not open-ended brainstorming — it answers a specific question. Examples:
   - "Why are enterprise users not using the reporting feature?"
   - "What manual work are our users doing that we could automate?"
   - "What adjacent problem does our product ignore that it could address?"

2. **Choose a research method.** Match the method to the question:
   - **User interviews** — for understanding behaviour and motivation
   - **Usage analytics review** — for identifying patterns and drop-off points
   - **Competitive analysis** — for understanding what alternatives exist and what users choose instead
   - **Jobs-to-be-done mapping** — for identifying the underlying job a user is hiring the product to do
   Use at least two methods. Single-method discovery produces biased conclusions.

3. **Conduct research.** For user interviews: run at least five sessions with users who represent the target population. Listen for workarounds — they reveal unmet needs. For analytics: look for unexpected patterns, not just confirmation of existing beliefs.

4. **Synthesise findings.** Group observations into themes. For each theme:
   - What is the observed behaviour or problem?
   - What need or goal does it suggest?
   - How frequently does it appear (pervasive vs. edge case)?
   - What is the cost to the user of the current situation?

5. **Generate opportunity statements.** For each significant theme, write an opportunity statement:
   "We have observed [evidence]. This suggests users need [inferred need]. If we addressed this, the impact could be [estimated value]."
   An opportunity statement is not a solution — it describes the problem space.

6. **Prioritise opportunities.** Score each opportunity on: frequency (how many users are affected), severity (how much it matters to them), and feasibility (how addressable it is given current capabilities).

7. **Recommend next steps.** For the top opportunities: recommend whether to proceed to requirements gathering, run a further spike, or park for later review.

## Output

- **Discovery question** — the specific question this research answered
- **Research summary** — methods used, users interviewed, data reviewed
- **Opportunity themes** — synthesised findings grouped by theme
- **Opportunity statements** — one per significant theme
- **Prioritised opportunity list** — ranked by frequency × severity × feasibility
- **Recommended next steps** — what to do with the top opportunities
